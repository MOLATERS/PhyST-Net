\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
This section presents the empirical evaluation of PhyST-Net. The experimental design is specified in detail because the evaluation protocol is part of the scientific contribution. A TKDE-style evaluation should not only report whether a method obtains lower error, but also explain under which data conditions, forecast horizons, graph scales, and computational budgets the method is effective.

The experiments are organized around six questions. \textbf{Q1:} Does PhyST-Net improve forecasting accuracy over temporal-only, Transformer-based, graph-based, and domain-specific baselines? \textbf{Q2:} Are the gains consistent across ultra-short, short, and medium horizons? \textbf{Q3:} Which modules contribute to the final performance, and are their effects dataset-dependent? \textbf{Q4:} How sensitive is the model to token number, graph sparsity, hidden dimension, and future-ExG modulation strength? \textbf{Q5:} What is the accuracy--efficiency tradeoff compared with competitive baselines? \textbf{Q6:} Do visualizations of learned relations and forecast cases support the proposed interpretation of temporal continuity, graph adaptation, future-driver conditioning, and feasibility-aware decoding?

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp_setup}
\textbf{Datasets.} The final evaluation will use SDWPF, HoT, and Kelmarsh. SDWPF is a large-scale wind-farm dataset with 134 nodes at 15-minute resolution and is used to evaluate scalability on a relatively dense graph. HoT contains 21 nodes from a highland site at 10-minute resolution and is used to test performance under terrain-driven variability. Kelmarsh contains 6 nodes at 10-minute resolution and is used to examine high-fidelity node-level forecasting in a small graph. Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats} summarizes the planned dataset statistics. The final manuscript should additionally report the date range, number of samples after cleaning, available exogenous variables, rated capacity scale, and missing-value handling policy for each dataset.

\textbf{Protocol.} Each sample follows the input contract in Section~\ref{sec:problem_definition}. The historical target sequence is active power. Historical ExG contains measured exogenous variables and time features over the look-back window. Future ExG contains forecast-horizon exogenous variables and future time features. Recorded future environmental fields may be used as an NWP-like proxy when operational NWP is unavailable; in that case, the paper should state that the experiment evaluates the future-ExG interface under known future drivers rather than NWP-error robustness. All datasets should use chronological splits, and normalization statistics should be fitted only on the training partition. The split protocol should avoid random shuffling because it can leak seasonal and exogenous regimes across train and test partitions.

\textbf{Forecast horizons.} The evaluation should include ultra-short, short, and medium horizons. Ultra-short prediction mainly tests recent-state tracking and local temporal continuity. Short-horizon prediction tests whether future ExG and graph propagation improve forecasts over practical scheduling windows. Medium-horizon prediction stresses the stability of the model under longer driver uncertainty and accumulated temporal drift. This horizon-based analysis is important because a method may perform well at one lead time but fail when the dominant information source changes.

\textbf{Baselines.} The final comparison should include representative temporal, Transformer, graph, and domain-specific baselines under the same split and horizon protocol. Candidate groups include: recurrent or convolutional forecasters, PatchTST-style temporal Transformers, iTransformer-style variate Transformers, adaptive STGNNs, and recent network forecasting models with NWP or SCADA fusion. A paired vanilla configuration should also be retained to isolate the contribution of the proposed architectural changes. \textbf{Metrics.} The primary metrics are RMSE and MAE. $R^2$ is reported to quantify explained variance, and the grid-compliance score $\Lambda$ is reported when the rated-capacity scale is well defined:
\begin{equation}
\Lambda=\left(1-\frac{\mathrm{RMSE}}{P_{rated}}\right)\times100\%,
\end{equation}
where $P_{rated}$ denotes the rated capacity scale used by the dataset-specific evaluation. MAPE should be reported only if a clear masking rule is specified for near-zero targets. For operational interpretation, RMSE should be discussed together with rated capacity, and MAE should be used to describe average dispatch deviation.

The final analysis should discuss three aspects. First, it should compare PhyST-Net with temporal-only and graph-aware baselines to show whether the graph adapter contributes beyond temporal representation learning. Second, it should compare PhyST-Net with exogenous-variable-aware baselines to test whether the separate historical-ExG and future-ExG interface is useful. Third, it should analyze horizon-dependent behavior, especially whether short-horizon gains come from local dynamics and whether medium-horizon gains depend more heavily on future ExG. If the method is not best on all metrics, the analysis should state this directly and explain whether the difference is related to horizon length, dataset scale, or the bias introduced by physical constraints.

The ablation study should remove one module at a time while keeping the same training protocol. The expected variants are w/o AGSP, w/o R-STMF, w/o K-AMC, w/o PI-DCH, and a paired vanilla backbone. AGSP ablation tests whether continuous tokenization helps beyond fixed patching. R-STMF ablation tests whether the two-branch graph adapter is useful beyond temporal encoding. K-AMC ablation tests whether future ExG should modulate latent states rather than only be concatenated at input. PI-DCH ablation tests whether differentiable feasibility shaping affects both accuracy and physical validity.

The analysis should avoid claiming that every module is universally beneficial. A more credible interpretation is to identify which datasets and horizons benefit from each inductive bias. For example, AGSP may be most useful when ramp events are frequent, R-STMF may be more important in larger graphs, K-AMC may dominate when future exogenous variables are reliable, and PI-DCH may be most visible in high-power regimes.

\subsection{Parameter Sensitivity}
\label{sec:parameter_sensitivity}
\textbf{[Placeholder: Insert sensitivity curves or tables for the number of AGSP patches, graph top-$K$, hidden dimension, and K-AMC modulation strength.]}

This analysis should connect hyperparameters to model behavior. The number of AGSP patches controls temporal resolution: too few patches may underrepresent regime changes, while too many patches increase computation and may reintroduce noise. Graph top-$K$ controls sparsity of relational propagation: small $K$ may miss useful long-range relations, while large $K$ can dilute local structure and approach dense attention. Hidden dimension controls representation capacity and overfitting risk. K-AMC modulation strength controls the influence of future ExG; overly strong modulation may make the model sensitive to NWP errors, whereas overly weak modulation may reduce the benefit of forecast-horizon drivers.

A TKDE-style evaluation should report both accuracy and computational cost. The analysis should be linked to the complexity terms in Section~\ref{sec:complexity_analysis}; in particular, it should verify whether the sparse top-$K$ graph adapter reduces the empirical cost relative to dense graph attention.

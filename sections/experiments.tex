\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section evaluates PhyST-Net on industrial wind-power forecasting tasks where predictive accuracy and operational feasibility are both essential. To examine its ability to model physical constraints, geographic relations, and weather-driven forcing, we instantiate the framework on distributed wind-power generation networks. Following Section~\ref{sec:problem_definition}, each sample contains historical turbine-level targets, historical covariates, forecast-horizon exogenous drivers, and graph metadata. The prediction target is future active power. Unless otherwise stated, all errors are reported on the held-out test split after inverse transformation to physical units.

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}
\label{sec:experimental_protocol}

We evaluate two prediction-step settings for each wind-power dataset. These settings are chosen because exogenous forcing, graph propagation, and constraint-aware decoding are all expected to affect the forecast as the prediction length increases. The main comparison covers three wind-power datasets with different spatial scales: SDWPF, HoT, and Kelmarsh.

The dataset selection follows a deliberately constrained evaluation principle. The main benchmark is restricted to wind-power forecasting because wind generation simultaneously exposes the three modeling requirements targeted by PhyST-Net: turbine-level physical bounds, geographically structured propagation, and weather-driven future forcing. Within this domain, SDWPF provides a large industrial network for scalability and spatial interaction, HoT offers a medium-size farm with long chronological coverage and moderate missingness, and Kelmarsh supplies a smaller but exogenous-rich setting for testing whether the model can benefit from richer meteorological descriptors. This combination avoids mixing incomparable target semantics in the primary leaderboard while still covering different graph sizes, covariate dimensions, sampling intervals, and prediction-step lengths.

SDWPF is a Chinese wind-farm dataset from Shandong with 134 turbines sampled every 15 minutes over more than two years. The loader supports both the historical HDF5 files and the normalized CSV layout, including turbine-level power targets, SCADA measurements, numerical weather prediction (NWP) variables, and turbine positions. The current SDWPF configuration uses average wind speed and average wind direction as meteorological drivers, together with standardized turbine coordinates for spatial encoding. HoT is the Hill of Towie wind-farm dataset from Scotland, with 21 turbines, 192794 timestamps, five exogenous variables, and 10-minute records from 2020-01-01 to 2024-09-01; its target missing rate is 1.83\%. Kelmarsh is a UK wind-farm dataset with six turbines, 157536 timestamps, 12 exogenous variables, and 10-minute records from 2016-01-03 to 2018-12-31; the processed target matrix contains no missing values. All three datasets follow a common wind-task interface with target, SCADA, NWP, and position files.

All datasets are split chronologically to avoid temporal leakage. The first 70\% of samples are used for training, the next 10\% for validation and early stopping, and the final 20\% for testing. Missing target values are filled before scaling by forward filling followed by zero filling for any remaining entries; when available, the original missingness can still be retained as a mask. Standardization parameters for power targets are fitted on the training segment and reused for validation and testing. Meteorological variables are scaled consistently across historical SCADA and forecast-horizon NWP inputs, and turbine coordinates are standardized before entering the spatial module. Calendar encodings include month, day, weekday, hour, and the native intra-hour bucket for each sampling interval.

The repository also contains loaders and exploratory summaries for HK Rooftop PV and ASHRAE building load. These datasets are not included in the main leaderboard because their target semantics and operational envelopes differ from turbine-level active power. Instead, they are used in the ablation study to test whether the proposed modules remain useful when the same forecasting contract is instantiated on related constrained-energy tasks.

Table~\ref{tab:exp_design} summarizes the representative configurations used in the main comparison. HoT and Kelmarsh are sampled every 10 minutes, while SDWPF is sampled every 15 minutes. For 10-minute datasets, the two prediction lengths are $H=432$ and $H=1440$, corresponding to 3 and 10 days ahead, respectively. For SDWPF, the step counts are adjusted to the 15-minute sampling interval, yielding $H=288$ and $H=960$ for the same physical durations. This design reports the task by prediction steps while keeping the physical duration comparable across datasets.

PhyST-Net uses a 3-layer encoder, one decoder layer, hidden size 256, feed-forward size 512, eight attention heads, dropout 0.1, and Adam optimization. Training is capped at 50 epochs with early stopping patience 5 and gradient clipping at 1.0. The loss combines mean-squared prediction error with a high-output penalty weighted by 2.0 to emphasize rated-power regimes. In the full model, AGSP, R-STMF, and K-AMC are enabled throughout the main comparison. The ablation scripts keep the same data split, optimizer, prediction length, and model width, changing only the target module switch.

We compare PhyST-Net with strong recent time-series and spatio-temporal baselines, including TimeXer, CrossFormer, PatchTST, iTransformer, Amplifier, DAG, DUET, and TimeKAN. For models that do not natively consume exogenous wind variables, we use a lightweight convolutional exogenous adapter so that all methods are evaluated with the same historical-power and future-NWP information. The primary metrics are mean absolute error (MAE) and root mean square error (RMSE), where lower values indicate better predictive accuracy.

\subsection{Main Results}
\label{sec:main_results}

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} summarizes the representative benchmark results. PhyST-Net achieves the best MAE in all six settings and the best RMSE in four of them. The gains are most pronounced on Kelmarsh, where PhyST-Net reduces MAE by 30.99\% at $H=1440$ and 25.05\% at $H=432$ relative to the strongest competing baseline. On SDWPF, it also maintains clear MAE reductions of 21.44\% at $H=960$ and 14.06\% at $H=288$. On HoT, PhyST-Net gives the lowest MAE for both prediction lengths, while DAG and TimeKAN remain competitive in RMSE. Overall, the results indicate that PhyST-Net is most effective when forecasting requires non-local spatial interaction and future exogenous information rather than near-term autoregressive continuity alone.

\subsection{Ablation Study Across Multiple Domains}
\label{sec:ablation}

To assess the contribution of each module across forecasting contexts, we conduct ablation studies on ASHRAE building energy, HK Rooftop PV, and SDWPF wind power. These datasets stress different aspects of the model: ASHRAE reflects building-level load dynamics with strong diurnal structure, HK Rooftop PV captures weather-driven solar variability, and SDWPF represents industrial wind power with rich inter-turbine dependencies. Together, they test adaptive patching, relational manifold modeling, and exogenous conditioning under diverse operating regimes.

Tables~\ref{tab:ablation_ashrae}--\ref{tab:ablation_sdwpf} report the full model and three controlled variants. The anchor is the complete PhyST-Net configuration. The w/o AGSP variant removes adaptive Gaussian-soft-windowed patching, w/o K-AMC removes KAN-enhanced adaptive meteorological conditioning, and w/o R-STMF removes relational spatio-temporal manifold fusion. We report MAE, RMSE, WAPE, and $R^2$ for each dataset.

Across the three datasets, the anchor model achieves the best or near-best performance on most metrics. \subsection{Discussion of the Evaluation Scope}
\label{sec:evaluation_scope}

The benchmark results show that PhyST-Net is strongest in the evaluated multi-step prediction regimes, where future meteorological drivers and spatial propagation have sufficient time to influence the target trajectory. We therefore emphasize the operating regime most aligned with the design of PhyST-Net: constrained graph forecasting under exogenous forcing.

The ablation evidence spans multiple domains and shows that adaptive patching, relational manifold modeling, and exogenous modulation improve forecasting across building energy, rooftop PV, and wind-power tasks. These results complement the cross-dataset wind-power comparison on SDWPF, HoT, and Kelmarsh.
